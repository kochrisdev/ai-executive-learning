# Lesson 4.3 — Bias, Fairness & Responsibility

**Time:** ~15 minutes

---

## How Bias Enters AI

AI models are trained on human-generated data — and human-generated data reflects human biases. This means:

- Underrepresented groups may receive lower-quality outputs
- Historical patterns (who gets hired, who gets loans) may be reinforced
- Cultural assumptions embedded in language may skew responses
- Models may perform differently across languages, regions, and demographics

This is not a theoretical concern. It has produced documented real-world harms in hiring tools, credit scoring, facial recognition, and content moderation.

---

## High-Risk Use Cases for Executive Decision-Makers

As an executive, you are not just a user — you are an authorizer. These are the use cases where bias creates legal and reputational exposure:

| Use Case | Specific Risk |
|---|---|
| **AI-assisted hiring** | Models trained on historical hiring data may encode gender, age, or race bias |
| **Credit / lending decisions** | Proxy variables may encode protected class discrimination |
| **Customer scoring and targeting** | Differential treatment of customer segments may violate consumer protection law |
| **Performance evaluation** | AI-assisted performance tools may systematically disadvantage certain groups |
| **Content moderation** | Inconsistent enforcement across demographic groups |

---

## The Accountability Question

When an AI-assisted decision causes harm — who is responsible?

The vendor is responsible for the model's behavior within their stated scope.
**You are responsible for the decisions you make using that model.**

### EU AI Act — Now in Force (2026)

The EU AI Act is no longer theoretical. The **August 2, 2026 deadline** marks full compliance requirements for high-risk AI systems. As an executive with EU customers or operations, you need to know:

| Risk Level | Examples | Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **Unacceptable (Banned)** | Social scoring, real-time biometric surveillance | Cannot be deployed in the EU |
| **High Risk** | AI in hiring, credit, education, critical infrastructure | Must register in EU database, maintain risk management systems, full documentation |
| **Limited Risk** | Chatbots, AI-generated content | Transparency obligations (disclose AI to users) |
| **Low / No Risk** | AI spam filters, recommendation systems | No specific requirements |

**Penalties:** Non-compliance with high-risk requirements → up to €15M or 3% of global revenue. Prohibited practices → up to €35M or 7% of global revenue.

**Questions every executive should be able to answer:**
1. Which of our products or services have EU customers?
2. Which AI components in those products meet the Act's definition of a "high-risk AI system"?
3. Is AI used in any employment, credit, or critical infrastructure decisions that affect EU persons?
4. Do we have a designated AI compliance owner?

Regulatory frameworks are also evolving in the US and UK — the direction of travel is toward more accountability, not less.

---

## Practical Guidance for Executives

1. **Never automate consequential individual decisions without human review.** Hiring, termination, credit, and legal matters require a human in the loop.

2. **Ask vendors the hard questions.** Before deploying AI in any people-facing process, ask: "What bias testing have you done? What are the known limitations? What are the failure modes?"

3. **Start with lower-risk use cases.** AI for drafting, research, and synthesis carries far less risk than AI for scoring, ranking, or deciding.

4. **Build oversight into workflows.** If AI is flagging or ranking people or opportunities, a human should review before action is taken.

5. **Document your AI use.** As regulations tighten, organizations that cannot demonstrate oversight will face greater scrutiny.

---

## Key Takeaway

Bias in AI is not a reason to avoid it — it is a reason to deploy it thoughtfully. The executives who get this right will build competitive advantages. The ones who ignore it will face legal, regulatory, and reputational consequences.
