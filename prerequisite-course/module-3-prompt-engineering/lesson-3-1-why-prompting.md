# Lesson 3.1 — Why Prompting Is a Leadership Skill

**Time:** ~10 minutes

---

## The Quality Gap Is Almost Always the Prompt

When executives say "AI didn't work for me," the failure is almost never the model. It is the prompt. The model is a powerful engine — but it needs clear direction.

Consider the difference:

**Weak prompt:**
> "Write something about our Q3 strategy."

**Strong prompt:**
> "You are a senior strategy consultant. Write a 1-page executive briefing on our Q3 strategic priorities for distribution to our top 20 investors. Tone: confident, concise, data-driven. Structure: key priorities, progress indicators, risks and mitigations. Assume the reader has seen our Q2 results."

The second prompt produces a usable output in one pass. The first requires multiple rounds of correction and is often abandoned.

---

## Why This Is a Leadership Skill

Prompting is fundamentally the skill of **clear thinking made explicit**. To prompt well, you must:

1. Know what outcome you actually want
2. Understand who the audience is
3. Specify constraints (length, format, tone, assumptions)
4. Provide the context a smart person would need

These are the same skills that make a good executive brief, a good manager's assignment, and a good board presentation. Prompting is not a technical skill — it is a communication skill.

---

## The Mindset Shift

| Old Mindset | New Mindset |
|---|---|
| "I'll see what it comes up with" | "I direct it toward a specific outcome" |
| "Fix it after" | "Specify correctly upfront" |
| "It should just know" | "I give it everything it needs" |
| "One try" | "Iteration is expected and fast" |

The executive who prompts well gets 10x more value from the same tools as the executive who prompts carelessly. This is a genuine productivity differentiator.
